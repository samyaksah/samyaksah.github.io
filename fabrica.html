CONTEXT

What is "What If?"

This project was developed as a post-launch microsite to archive the work produced for the What If? exhibition, hosted by the Italian Art and Cultural Centre, Fabrica. The exhibition houses the works of 12 international artists at the end of their art residency and explores the infinite possibilities of our future.

MICROSITE BREAKDOWN

Encapsulate the Physical Experience

The content strategy for the microsite was to encapsulate the physical experience of the exhibition by creating a sense of immersion for visitors. This was accomplished by pacing content through the use of distortion techniques, denial & reward, and resistive scrolling. To further elicit a sense of immersion, each exhibit was given its own unique identity.

Following is the full walkthough if the microsite. I will be breaking down the reel for in-depth showacase.

Setting the Tone

The landing and introductory pages act as primers to get visitors accustomed to our use of distortion to reveal content. As visitors scroll, they will undistort and unveil information that contextualizes Fabrica and What If?

Selection Page

Acting as the focal point of the microsite, the exhibit selection page helps visitors make a decision on which exhibits to explore. This is accomplished through the use of denial and reward in both the image cropping and the “what if” prompts.

Individual Voices

Each exhibit page focuses on surfacing the unique identity of each piece and giving it its own voice. This is accomplished through the use of a unique color and font pairing and a custom interaction that is abstracted from the physical exhibit to elicit similar thoughts and emotions.

Tailored Interactions

Mirroring the physical experience, Faith’s exhibit page utilizes the visitor’s cursor, as an abstraction of themselves, to achieve the same effect and facilitate a similar introspection.

Fabricantes

Below the exhibit selection page is the Fabricante page, which is an open space where visitors can click anywhere to reveal the name and portrait of all the artists involved in the exhibition.

Persistent Navigation

The navbar is a dynamic site-wide navigation system that helps visitors with wayfinding. By anchoring a persistent visual element to the page, guests are able to better orient themselves when switching between the exhibit selection and exhibit pages.

Resistive Scroll

Carefully placed resisitive scroll puntuates the content across the site. Acting as a speed bump for the scrolling in order to create a more mindful reading experience.

PROCESS

Our Qualities and Principles

With Chris Ashworth (king of swiss grit) being our selected graphic designer to study, we started looking at his work primarily from Raygun to his design qualities. Ashworth sought to introduce a Swiss influence to the post-modern American grunge, resulting in a sharp and rugged aesthetic which he refers to as Swiss Grit.

Following are the design qualities that Ashworth utilizes in their work that we decided to explore in our graphic experimentation.

Our goal was not to copy their style, but to abstract qualities that can be designed into our form

These qualities were then combined with 2 design principles studied deeply and adapted from the famous graphic designer, curator, writer, critic, and educator Ellen Lupton’s book called, Graphic Design: The New Basics.

FINAL POSTER

Building From Sketches

Reinforcing our focus on qualities and principles, we continued to study and explore them by generating sketches. Then, by introducing content and combining different explorations of qualities and principles, these sketches began to form into varied avenues for further iteration.


Chosen Explorations

The most promising exploration we identified was one that made use of a varying grid to create progressive rhythm, making this structure apparent through the manipulation of type. In our lateral process of iteration, these were the characteristics that we chose to explore further.

I was heavily involved in the process of sketching as well as responsible for creating the assets that would go along with the posters.

MICROSITE DESIGN

Initial Exploration

A process of collecting and curating an extensive list of distortion techniques, interactions, animations, navigation, and methods of implementing dynamic grids were explored; formulating into three distinct approaches to surface content through interactivity.


3 Lines of Investigation

After several rounds of converging and diverging, we settled on these 3 lines of investigation ranging from semi-functional to a more expressive approach in term of art direction and surfacing of the content.

  • Grid Deconstruction - Pairing of dynamic grid system with methods of distortion in order to intuitively surface content.
  • Distortion as Guides - Directing attention to key elements, and providing a “route” through which to experience the microsite
  • Playful Agency - Blank canvas for visitors to poplulate as they see fit

Refining our Content strategy

To help us reframe our content strategy and let it integrate better into our final form, we did more grouping and found two key insights:

  • Giving each exhibit its own voice to surface content
  • Pacing content to create a more immersive experience

REFLECTION

New Experiences and satisfied faces

Given the fact that we spent numerous hours conducting design sprint, interviewing customer, designing and redesigning the cards, display and signage as well as testing those on the customer, it is safe to say that we we are very satisfied with the final product we came up with. The project really gave me a new insight into interaction and service design. Understanding the customers and their need was initially challenging but the more interviews we did, the easier it got.

We believe that our project project is meaningful for our target audience, , the staff members feel less anxious approaching the customer, customer has the freedom to browse independently or use the cards to ask for the staff's recommendation and the store owner gets more staff and customer interaction and with the satisfaction of creating a returning customer that would increase revenue.

But that's not to say that this is the perfect solution, I am sure that there is a lot more user testing and prototyping to do. Ideally we would like to have our final product be a part of the store for a couple weeks and see how the product fares. I am sure that we will find new problems that would require creative solutions and I believe this is whats exciting about being a designer.
