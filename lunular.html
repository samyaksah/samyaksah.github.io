Lunular is an AR Based try-on tool for users who have a hard time deciding on the perfect nail-color that would suit them. The app also serves as a place to find trustworthy and reputable salons that are worth the price.


This is an 8-week product design project where my team and I designed an app that implements Augmented Reality to reduce cognitive overload when selecting nail colors and designs.

PROBLEM

Theory of Reasoned Action

Created by Martin Fishbein and Icek Ajzen in the late 1960s, the Theory of Reasoned Action states that a consumer only takes a specific action when there is an equally specific result expected. From the time the consumer decides to act to the time the action is completed, the consumer retains the ability to change his or her mind and decide on a different course of action.
We as a team found that some businesses are finding it harder to promote their products as users want to be validated in their purchases before they actually buy it. These businesses tend to be in the area where customers historically can only go in-store to buy the products that would fit their preferences, like the jewelry, nails, or clothing industry. Our focus on the nail industry was backed up by our initial research where nail enthusiasts felt that drop-in appointment wait times are too long, with no clear catalog of options, or an overwhelming amount of customization choices and not a lot of time to choose.

RESEARCH PROCESS

Understanding the User

With our long term goal in mind, we set out to scope into our target demographics and flesh out their needs and pain points. We created a persona for our customers to reflect on our insight so far, which would also serve as a metric to test our successful design solutions. Since AR is supposed to be a major component of the interface, we decided to limit our persona to tech-savvy nail enthusiasts.

Jessica Wilson - Persona for Lunular

Understanding the "while in-salon" process

We set out to understand more about the customer and their experiences when it comes to getting a manicure, I conducted several interviews with avid nail enthusiasts who either have a lot of experience with manicures or have recently started to get one. The goal was to learn more about their needs, motives, goals and pain points when inside the salon. What is their thought process when it comes to selecting a design?

insights

From an in-depth analysis of the interviews, we noticed that customers were overwhelmed when selecting a color on the spot from a catalog of hundreds of colors. Getting nails done professionally is costly and the selected color stays on the nails for a very long time, which is why the decision has to be perfect.

While the cognitive overload when selecting a color was one of their major concerns, they also had issues with the trustworthiness of the salon itself. Generally, salons don’t put out a price list of their services online for the customer to review before they choose a salon, which makes the decision objectively more difficult.

According to the interviews, the price of the service and the proximity of the salon are two major factors for the user when it comes to choosing a salon. Customers also factor in their past experiences and the competence of the nail technician to get the job done.

We saw an opportunity to facilitate the decision-making process for the customers before they enter the salon to reduce cognitive overload and objectively improve the in-store experience for them.

“Help customers feel confident and satisfied with their nail colour and design selections.”

I proposed a long term goal of reducing the pressure and stress that comes with selecting from a multitude of different options. Inspired by recent developments in the AR industry, we felt that incorporating augmented reality into the nail designing process (by projecting customized designs onto the user’s nails captured by their phone camera screen) could help nail enthusiasts make a more informed choice before an expensive appointment with salons. AR could potentially work as a quick prototyping tool that can allow users to try out different designs or colors that could match their skin tone or unique style.

Understanding Augmented Reality

Using Augmented reality as a try-on tool was the main selling point of our app, which is why I took on the responsibility to learn more about the intricacies of AR and how we can implement that in our app. I scoured the internet and talked to graduate researchers from whom I learned about the value of trackers and Z-buffering and how existing cameras may take a long time to track a target without a marker. Even though the technology is not yet refined to be implemented in our app, my research allowed us to simulate the experience as closely as possible through mockups and animations

Lessons from other domains

In Neilson/norman group’s 10 Usability Heuristics for User Interface Design, one of the principles is about matching the system with the real world. Since we wanted our app to co-exist with other photo/video editing apps that people generally use, I turned to apps like Snapseed and VSCO for inspiration. Snapseed’s tools dock design is clean and can fit quite a number of tools and filters in it, which inspired me to implement that in our app. Similarly, we took inspiration from Snapchat for the UI of our AR Feature. Users can swipe left and right to select nail designs that have been saved in the catalog previously.

Pinterest has a handy tap and hold interaction that can be used to save, share and hide pins. Because of how it decreases clutter on the screen, I used this interaction for our app where users could select which finger they want to apply the color on. This worked well in the context of our app by decreasing color and increasing how quickly users can make a selection.

Low Fidelity tests, iterations and it’s challenges

I was responsible for creating the first draft of the prototype which we as a team could iterate on. I started with creating a basic interface on Adobe XD for all the features of the app factoring in the insights from the user studies. This app puts the user on the front seat when it comes to customizing their nails which interactions like drag, zoom pinch.

While creating prototypes for the app, one major challenge we faced was finding the right prototyping tool that could support the animation we wanted for our app’s customization tools to do. We initially used Adobe XD which supported simple transitions but could not fully translate the customization tool’s ability during some user testing. I then set out to create a much more interactive prototype on Axure to conduct a more fruitful user testing session with our target audience.

Another team member and I conducted several user testing sessions and were able to find some user experience and efficiency issues. We knew the limitations of the prototype which is why we were more interested in their reactions on the quality of the iconography, our use of skeuomorphism to make the tool dock intuitive, the salon finder and the overall enjoyment of the app.

REFLECTION

More Research and More Users tests

With almost over 56,300 individual nail salons and annual revenue of $5.29bn USD, the nail industry is booming. Our app proposal, although limited in its scope, can help organize the chaos that every nail enthusiast goes through when they want to get their nails done. With this app, we wanted to open paths to several other retail avenues where AR could be used to make users’ lives easier.

If given more time to iterate, I believe it’s important for our team to conduct more user interviews with salon owners to get their insights and create an app that both stakeholders (customers and salon owners) can enjoy.
